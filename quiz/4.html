Part 1: TikTok Troubles

TikTok can track all of your activities on the Internet, including your passwords.

Research from a German data protection research agency (Rufposten) revealed in 2019 that TikTok installs browser trackers on your device which track all your activities on the internet. In 2020, Apple caught TikTok spying on millions of iPhone users, including logging passwords on other apps.

TikTok can can track your activity on totally unrelated, really personal websites.

According to Consumer Reports, TikTok embeds tracking on hundreds of other websites. "If you go to the United Methodist Church's main website, TikTok hears about it. Interested in joining Weight Watchers? TikTok finds that out, too. The Arizona Department of Economic Security tells TikTok when you view pages concerned with domestic violence or food assistance. Even Planned Parenthood uses the trackers, automatically notifying TikTok about every person who goes to its website, though it doesn't share information from the pages where you can book an appointment."

TikTok's privacy practices are so bad that they've been sued in court for violating privacy!

In October 2022, TikTok users who created videos on the app before September 30, 2021 began receiving payments up to $167.04 after a $92 million class-action data privacy settlement. Would you be eligible under that lawsuit?

Do you think the risks are worth it? The decision is up to you.

Even if you don't want to delete TikTok, there's a lot you can do to protect your privacy.

The Washington Post recommends that to limit how much advertisers can target you, go to Settings and Privacy → Privacy → Ads Personalization. Then, make sure that 'Use of Off-TikTok Activity for Ad Targeting' is turned off and gray.
